Four views of the same proposal

You work in 2D and you decide in 3D. The switch buttons are on the map, and you never redraw a proposal — it's the same intervention, seen four ways.

2D

The map

Selecting parcels, drawing, boundaries, ownership. Everything that gets made, gets made here.

The 3D view

Rotate the view by holding Ctrl and dragging with the mouse (on a phone: swipe with two fingers). The wheel zooms.

View controls

Building rendering You choose what is visible — Built, Planned, Both or Neither — and how: Solid, Transparent, Surviving or Off.
Hide real buildings under proposals Removes the existing buildings your proposal replaces anyway, so they don't stand inside one another.
Radius How much of the surrounding city gets loaded. Smaller radius = faster view.
Show all parcels Draws the parcel boundaries in 3D as well.
Trees Turns the greenery on or off.
For a before-and-after comparison, set Built to Transparent and Planned to Solid. You immediately see what the intervention adds and what it removes.

What the proposal is worth

Click a parcel in 3D and a panel with the arithmetic opens. It compares Built and Proposed by Volume and Floor area, and the Price per m² slider lets you set your assumption about the market.

The result is a single number: Proposal gain — how much the parcel gains or loses. If there is no proposal, the panel only shows the Current value.

Clicking show next to the proposal's name isolates the whole proposal. The panel then switches to Proposal info: the number of parcels, the total gain and the Avg / parcel.

The average per parcel is often more important than the total gain. A proposal with a big total but a poor average means a handful of parcels are carrying all of it — and the other owners have no reason to say yes.

Realistic context

The button drops your buildings into Google's photorealistic 3D model of the city. It is the best test of “would this look insane here?” — the neighbouring structures, their heights and their roofs are the real ones. Where no photorealistic data exists for the area, you get satellite imagery and terrain instead.

Walk

The button puts you at eye level. What looks spacious in plan can turn out cramped on the street — and the other way round. The button only exists in cities for which walk mode has been set up.

Before you share a proposal, check it in all three: 2D for boundaries and ownership, 3D for volume and gain, realistic context for whether it fits the city as it actually is.
