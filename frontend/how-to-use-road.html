You draw a road, you don't describe it

Roads and tracks are drawn straight onto the map, like in a city-building game. The alignment cuts the parcels it passes through and creates new blocks on either side — the basis for everything you do afterwards. The proposal only comes once you're happy with the line.

  1. Start the drawing tool

    Select a parcel, open the Proposals tab and, next to the “Build” palette, click Draw Road or Draw Track.

    Roads are deliberately not part of the “Build” palette: they aren't built on the selected parcel, they cut across it. The tool is only available in cities that have the road tools enabled.

  2. Draw the alignment

    Click along the map to place the points of the line. The Draw road panel opens and tells you live what you are doing:

    Road Width From 2 to 7 metres. For wider profiles with lanes and sidewalks, use the cross-section (next step).
    Road Length and Road Area Computed as you draw.
    Parcels Intersected How many parcels the line cuts, and who owns them (individuals, companies, the government).
    TEAD Total Estimated Acquiring Difficulty — how much pain buying the land will cost you. Smaller is better.

    Take back a bad point with Undo (U). Complete the line with Finish Road (F), or walk away with Cancel Road (Esc).

    TEAD in practice: land owned by the government and by institutions costs nothing in difficulty, companies carry a coefficient of 1, and private individuals 2. A line that skirts around private parcels is far likelier to actually get built, even if it runs a little longer.

  3. Edit the cross-section

    Click Edit cross-section. The Cross-section window opens, and you assemble the road out of lanes — like an actual engineer.

    There is no width slider, and that is deliberate. Total width is the sum of the lanes, not a setting: add a lane and the road widens, remove one and it narrows. You change the width by changing the lanes.
    • Standard cross-sections — one click gives you a whole correct profile: Alley, Local, Collector, Main street, Avenue or Boulevard. Start here, then tweak.
    • Add lane — a palette of types: traffic lane, sidewalk, cycle path, parking, green verge, median, bus lane. Each arrives at its standard width, inserted right next to the lane you have selected.
    • Drag a lane by its handle to reorder it.
    • Change a width by hand and a ↺ appears to put it back to standard.
    • A green strip can be Grass only or a Tree grove.
    • Applies to — This segment or Entire road network.

    There is one special gesture: drag a seam between two lanes in the diagram and width transfers from one to the other. The total stays put, so the alignment does not move — that is how you rearrange a profile inside a right-of-way you already have.

    Confirm with Apply to drawing. A road cannot exceed 80 m; push past it and the app tells you, instead of silently refusing the change.

    The editor warns you when the profile hits buildings or cuts applied parks, squares and lakes. To tunnel through buildings, use drawing mode.

  4. Tune a built road

    Once the road is built you can still change it. Click it and work directly on the map:

    • Nodes — drag to move, ⌥-click to remove.
    • Junctions — dragging moves all the legs at once; ⌥-click disconnects them.
    • Connecting — draw up to an existing road and the two become one.
    • Bulldozing — 🚜 Bulldoze this stretch.
    A network behaves like a network: remove a stretch in the middle and the road comes apart into two separate ones.

  5. Turn the road into a proposal

    A built road is not yet a proposal — so far it exists only for you. Click it, then hit Propose in its panel. Create Proposal opens, where you enter the name, the description and the offer to the owners of the intersected parcels. The acquisition strategy is partial — a road only needs a strip through the parcel, not the whole parcel.

    The same panel holds Edit cross-section and Share, plus Unapply and Delete if you change your mind.

The other way in: a constrained corridor

If you have already selected parcels and, in the Create Proposal window, picked Road/Track as the goal, the Constrained corridor tool opens instead — drawing limited to those parcels.

  • Full parcel — the corridor is the entire outline of the selected parcels; you draw nothing.
  • Draw — you draw the line inside them, choosing from preset widths that run from Alley ~7.5 m to Boulevard ~80 m, plus the sidewalk width.

The parcels must be contiguous, otherwise you get “Parcels must be contiguous to draw a constrained corridor.” Confirm with Done.

A new road splits existing parcels and creates new blocks on both sides. That is why a road is almost always the first move: only once you know where the blocks are does it make sense to propose buildings, parks or land readjustment.
