Build first, propose second

Consensus Builder works like a city-building game. You don't start by filling in a form — you start by picking land and building something on it. Only once the design looks the way you want it do you write the terms, and that turns it into a proposal other people can decide on.

1. The workflow

Select One parcel, several, or a whole block
Build Click a tool in the palette
Design Shape it, tune the parameters
Terms Name, description, offer, ownership
Share Apply it, look at it, send it

  1. Select land on the map

    Click a parcel. For anything spanning several parcels, turn on Select multiple parcels and click them one by one. The parcel panel opens — go to the Proposals tab.

    If you select a single parcel that sits inside a block, the app will ask: “Only one parcel is selected. Did you mean the whole block (N parcels)?” Choose Yes, select the block and it selects the whole block for you.

  2. Click a tool in the “Build” palette

    Every tool drops you straight into its designer — no intermediate step. Buildings, land readjustment and freeform open an editor; Park, Square and Lake are applied in a single click. The full list of tools is in the next section.

  3. Design it

    In the editor you change the shape, the typology, the number of floors and the rest of the parameters. You see the result on the map as you work. When you're happy, confirm the design — usually with Done.

  4. Write the terms

    Once the design is done, the Create Proposal window opens. This is where everything the design can't say goes: Name, Description, Offer, and who receives ownership. Under Options you can add conditionality, an expiry, offer decay or a deposit.

  5. Apply it and look at it

    The proposal exists, but the map doesn't change until you explicitly apply it. In the proposal details, click Apply to map, then look at it in 3D before you share it.

2. The proposal palette

The palette lives in the Proposals tab of the parcel panel and shows the spatial and ownership proposals available for the current selection.

Tool What it does
Block Fills the block with buildings from an urban rule (buildable envelope, height, setbacks). Buildings guide →
Row houses A terrace of attached houses across the selected parcels. Needs at least two parcels.
Detached One standalone house on each selected parcel.
Freeform A building you draw yourself, no rules — for when the typologies don't fit.
Land readjustment Pools the selected parcels and re-divides them into a tidier layout. Guide →
Park A park across the selected parcels, in one click. You add the furniture (trees, benches, paths) afterwards. Guide →
Square A public square, in one click. It comes with a statue; fountains, tables and benches you add afterwards.
Lake A body of water across the selected parcels. The parcels must be contiguous.
Roads, tracks and stations share the Transport group. Road and Track sit in their own framed family because they aren't built on parcels — they cut through them. Roads and tracks guide →
Ownership comes last. The Offer tool has its own category because it changes purchase or ownership terms without changing the map.
Block, Row houses and Detached are rules — the shape falls out of the parameters you set. Freeform is the opposite: you draw exactly what you want. If a rule is boxing you in, switch to freeform.

3. Drafts

A design you didn't finish is not lost. It is saved as a Draft and waits for you in the drafts list — resume it later, edit it, or discard it.

The draft editor has Design, Parcels, Ownership, Terms and Details tabs, so you can work on each part of the proposal separately. Review changes shows you exactly what changes, and Create proposal publishes it.

A published proposal is immutable. Publishing from a draft creates a new proposal — the original is neither changed nor deleted. Editing is therefore always a matter of creating a new version, never of patching the old one.

4. Views

You can look at the same proposal in four ways. The buttons are on the map, top right.

2D

The map

For selecting parcels, drawing, and checking boundaries. This is where the work happens.

3D

The spatial model

Buildings, roads and public space as 3D objects — plus a panel that works out what the proposal is worth to you.

Realistic context

Your proposal standing inside Google's photorealistic model of the city.

Walk

Eye level, from a pedestrian's point of view. Only in cities that support it.

5. Sharing

One proposal

Open the proposal and click Share. You get a link that opens exactly that intervention.

The whole plan

Apply every proposal you want to send, then in the sidebar click Share entire plan (all proposals). The link preserves their order and their dependencies.

A second variant

Want to try an alternative? Copy the proposal into a new one instead of losing the existing one — both stay around, side by side.

Remember the order: select → build → design → terms → apply → share. When something confuses you, it is almost always because you are trying to describe an intervention before you have built it.
